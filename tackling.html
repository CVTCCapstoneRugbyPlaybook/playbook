Tackling

The Laws of tackling

Tackling is the only way of legally bringing down your opponent in rugby.



For safty reason, you cannot make contact above the sholders.
A penalty will be issued if a referee sees a high tackle.
A yellow card and a spell in the sin-bin or a red card and instant dismissal may be given for more serious offences.



Going to the Ground

Once a player in possession of the ball has been brought to ground by a tackler, they must release the ball immediately.
This can done by either by passing off to a team-mate or placing the ball on the ground.
If they do not release the ball immediately and roll away from it, they will concede a penalty.

The tackler must release the player they have just brought down and roll away from them and the ball.
If the referee believes the tackler has not rolled away quick enough, he will award a penalty to the opposition.

Stealing the ball in the tackle

A team-mate of the tackler can pick up the ball from the contact area as long as they are on their feet.
As soon as a team-mate from the ball carrier's side comes into contact with that player and the ball is still on the ground, the tackle then becomes a ruck.
None of the tackler's team-mates can attempt to handle or pick up the ball once the ruck has formed.

Other laws concerning tackling

If a player has been tackled and their natural momentum takes them over the try-line and the ball is grounded, a try is awarded.
A player tackled near the goal-line can also reach out and attempt to touch the ball down for a try.
If the ball carrier has been held by an opponent, but has not gone to ground, and a team-mate has bound onto them, a maul is formed. At that point a tackle cannot be made for safety reasons.
